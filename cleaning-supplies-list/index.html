Cleaning Supplies List for a Cleaning Business — the Complete Starter Kit

Everything you actually need to clean your first paying house — by category, with a realistic budget and the things you should not buy yet. This is step 5 of starting a house cleaning business, expanded into a shopping list you can print and take to the store.

The core caddy — what goes room to room

One sturdy caddy holding everything you touch every hour. If it doesn't fit in the caddy, it stays in the car.

  • Cleaning caddy with a handleThe system, not just a container: same spot for everything, nothing left behind in a client's bathroom.
  • Microfiber cloths — at least 24The single most-used item in this business. Color-code by zone (e.g. blue = glass, yellow = bathroom) so you never wipe a counter with a toilet cloth.
  • All-purpose cleanerYour default for counters, tables, spot-cleaning walls, most surfaces.
  • Glass & mirror cleanerStreak-free matters — mirrors are one of the first things clients inspect.
  • Bathroom / disinfecting cleanerToilets, sinks, showers. A separate labeled bottle — never the all-purpose one.
  • Degreaser or kitchen cleanerStovetops, range hoods, backsplashes. All-purpose spray gives up on cooked-on grease.
  • Toilet bowl cleaner + brushMany pros bring their own brush per job or use disposable heads — clients notice (and love) this.
  • Scrub sponges & non-scratch padsToss and replace often; a worn sponge is a smell risk and a scratch risk.
  • Detail brushes (an old toothbrush works)Faucet bases, grout lines, tracks of sliding doors — the details that earn 5-star reviews.
  • Razor scraper (plastic + glass-safe)Paint specks, sticker residue, dried-on gunk on glass and ceramic.

Tools & equipment — the bigger buys

  • Vacuum (reliable, light enough for stairs)Your biggest single purchase. Corded uprights and quality refurbished machines are fine to start — bagged models handle client-home dust better than you'd think.
  • Flat mop with washable pads + bucketMicrofiber flat mops beat string mops for speed and streaks on most modern floors.
  • Broom & dustpanQuick kitchen sweeps and entryways before mopping.
  • Extendable dusterCeiling fans, vents, cobwebs in corners — without a ladder.
  • Step stool (folding)Above-fridge and top-shelf dust is where deep cleans are won; standing on a client's chair is how deposits are lost.
  • Spray bottles (labeled) if you buy concentrateConcentrate + your own bottles is the cheapest cost-per-job by far.

Safety & consumables — restock these constantly

  • Nitrile gloves (box)Fresh pair per house minimum — it protects you and looks professional.
  • Trash bags (kitchen + small sizes)Replacing liners is expected on most jobs; carrying your own means never hunting through a client's cabinets.
  • Paper towelsFor the gross stuff you don't want in your microfiber laundry.
  • Shoe covers or dedicated indoor shoesA tiny cost that instantly signals "professional" at the front door.
  • Knee pads or a kneeling matBaseboards and tubs are a knees business. Protect yours — you're the whole company.
  • Caddy sparesExtra sponges, a backup spray head (they die mid-job), a lint roller for upholstery touch-ups.

What NOT to buy yet

  • A carpet extractor or floor machine — rent one the first few times a job genuinely needs it, and charge for it. Buy only when rentals cost you more than a machine would.
  • A steam cleaner — great tool, not a day-one tool. Add it once recurring clients justify it.
  • Bulk chemical cases — you don't know your usage rate yet, and you may switch products after a month of real jobs. Buy singles, then buy concentrate in bulk once you know what you like.
  • Branded uniforms or a wrapped vehicle — a clean polo and a magnetic car sign come first; full branding comes after your name has survived a few months of real use.
  • Specialty products for surfaces you haven't met (granite polish, wood oil, stainless wipes) — buy them when a client's home actually calls for them, and note it on their intake form so it's in the kit next visit.

How the pros keep supply costs low

Buy concentrate, not ready-to-use

Ready-to-use spray bottles are mostly water at a huge markup. Concentrated all-purpose cleaner and disinfectant diluted into your own labeled bottles can cut your chemical cost per job dramatically. Follow the dilution ratio on the label — stronger is not cleaner, it just leaves residue and burns through product.

Treat microfiber as an asset, not a consumable

Good microfiber survives hundreds of washes if you treat it right: wash separately (or with lint-free items), skip fabric softener and dryer sheets entirely (they coat the fibers and kill the static that makes microfiber work), and dry low or air-dry. A color-code system plus a mesh laundry bag per color keeps the sorting effortless.

Track supply cost per job from day one

Weigh your first month honestly: chemicals, replaced sponges, gloves, trash bags, gas. Most solo cleaners land somewhere around a few dollars per standard clean — but your number is the one that belongs in your quotes. When supplies creep up, your price follows, calmly and with evidence.

Your kit list is also your deep-clean pitch

When a client sees the extendable duster and the grout brush come out, upselling a deep clean stops being a sales pitch and starts being obvious. Carry the tools for the service one tier above what was booked.

Should I use the client's products instead?

Some clients prefer it (scent sensitivities, favorite brands) and it saves you money — but your results become only as good as whatever's under their sink. Most pros bring their own kit by default and happily switch to client-supplied products on request, noted on the intake form. Either way, decide the policy before your first job so your quote reflects it.

Do I need "professional" products, or are store brands fine?

Start with what's on the shelf at any big-box store — plenty of successful cleaners never leave it. Janitorial-supply concentrate becomes worth it when volume makes the per-job savings real, usually once you're cleaning daily. The vacuum is the one place where quality shows up immediately in results and in your back.

What about green / non-toxic products?

Genuine demand exists — some clients will book you because of it. If you go this route, go honestly: use certified products, say which ones, and put it in your business name or tagline (see the eco section of our name ideas). Note that some disinfecting jobs (post-illness, mold, rentals) may still call for conventional products — say so up front.

Never mix which chemicals?

Bleach + ammonia (or bleach + many acidic cleaners, including some vinegar-based ones) produces toxic gas. The simplest safe rule for a working cleaner: one product per surface, rinse between products, and never combine "to make it stronger." Keep products in their original labeled bottles or clearly labeled spray bottles — it's a safety issue and, in some states, a labeling requirement.

How often do I replace things?

Sponges: every few jobs or at the first smell. Microfiber: when it stops grabbing (slides instead of drags). Mop pads: wash after every house, replace when the fibers flatten. Vacuum filters: check monthly — a clogged filter makes a good vacuum clean like a bad one. Spray heads: keep a spare; they always die mid-job.
