Cleaning Service Quote Template

A free house cleaning quote you can copy, paste and send today — plus a plain-English breakdown of what a winning quote includes, so the client says yes instead of "let me think about it". No sign-up.

A quote does two jobs: it tells the client exactly what they'll get and what it costs, and it makes saying yes effortless. Most lost jobs aren't lost on price — they're lost to quotes that were slow, vague about scope, or ended without a clear next step. Here's what a good one includes. What a cleaning quote should include

  1. Your business details. Business name, phone, email. If you're insured, say so here — "licensed & insured" wins tie-breaks against cheaper quotes.
  2. A quote number, date, and expiry. "Valid for 14 days" protects you from honoring a price months later, and gives the client a gentle reason not to sit on it.
  3. The client and property. Who it's for and the address you're quoting. A price for a 2-bed apartment isn't a price for a 4-bed house — tie the number to the property.
  4. What you saw or were told. One line like "based on our walk-through on [date]" or "based on details you provided". If the home turns out very different, this line is why the price can change.
  5. Itemized scope of work. One line per service — "Standard clean: kitchen, 2 baths, floors, dusting", "Add-on: inside oven" — each with its price. Itemizing is what makes your quote comparable to nothing else the client received.
  6. What's NOT included. The two or three things people commonly assume: exterior windows, mold remediation, heavy lifting. One short exclusions line prevents the awkward on-site conversation later.
  7. The total — and the frequency. Make the price impossible to miss, and state whether it's one-time or per visit. If you offer a recurring discount ("$140 first clean, $110 weekly after"), show both numbers; recurring clients are the business.
  8. Terms in brief. Deposit if you take one, payment methods, your cancellation/reschedule window. Details live in your service agreement; the quote just previews the essentials.
  9. How to accept. The single most skipped line. "Reply YES to this email and we'll confirm [date]" — an exact, one-step action. Every extra step between "yes" and booked loses jobs.
  10. A friendly close. One human line ("We'd love to get your Saturdays back") — you're often the only quote that sounds like a person.

Quote fast — speed wins more jobs than price

When someone asks for a cleaning quote, they usually ask two or three businesses at once. The first professional, specific quote back sets the standard the others get compared to — and often gets the booking before the others reply at all. Aim to send a quote the same day you get the request. That's the real advantage of having a template: the hard thinking is already done, you're just filling in the property and the price (the free price calculator gets you a defensible number in under a minute).

Quote, estimate, or bid — which is which?

In residential cleaning the words blur, but roughly: an estimate is a ballpark ("usually $120–160 for a home like yours") — fine for a first phone call. A quote is a specific price for a specific scope, and that's what this template is. A bid usually means competitive commercial work. Lead with an estimate on the phone if you must, but get to a written quote fast — verbal numbers get misremembered, always downward.

Should I do a walk-through before quoting?

For recurring residential work, yes if you can — five minutes in the home beats any questionnaire, and "condition" is the variable that sinks profitability. Can't visit? Quote from photos or a video call, and lean on the "based on details you provided" line so a much-dirtier-than-described home is a price conversation, not a standoff. Collect the property details once with the free client intake form and quoting takes minutes.

After they say yes

Two documents finish the job the quote started: a simple service agreement so scope, access, and cancellation terms are agreed before the first visit, and a clean invoice when the work is done. Quote → agreement → invoice is the whole paper trail of a professional cleaning business.

Should I quote a flat rate or hourly?

Flat rate, for almost all residential work. Clients hate open-ended hourly ("will it be 3 hours or 5?"), and flat pricing pays you for being fast instead of penalizing it. Price the first visit higher (first cleans run long), then a steady per-visit rate. Hourly still makes sense for genuinely unpredictable jobs like hoarding cleanups or post-construction.

How long should a quote stay valid?

7–14 days is standard. Short enough that your costs won't drift and the client feels a nudge to decide, long enough not to feel like pressure. When one expires, a friendly "prices are holding if you're still interested" message often revives it.

What if the client says it's too expensive?

Reduce scope, not your rate: "I can hit that budget if we skip the interior windows and make it bi-weekly instead of weekly." An itemized quote makes this a two-minute negotiation — they can see exactly what each piece costs. Cutting your rate on request teaches clients the first number was padded.
