Cleaning Invoice Template

A free house cleaning invoice you can copy, paste and send today — plus a plain-English breakdown of everything a cleaning invoice needs so you look professional, keep clean records, and get paid on time. No sign-up.

An invoice does two jobs: it tells the client exactly what to pay and how, and it gives you a paper trail for taxes and disputes. Most late payments aren't clients refusing to pay — they're invoices that were vague, late, or hard to act on. Here's what a good one includes.

What a cleaning invoice should include

  1. Your business details. Business name, phone, email — and your logo if you have one. The client should recognize you at a glance and know how to reach you with questions.
  2. An invoice number and date. Any simple sequence works (e.g. 2026-014). Numbering makes your records searchable, looks professional, and matters at tax time.
  3. The client and service address. Who it's for and where the work happened — essential once you have more than a handful of clients or invoice a landlord for multiple properties.
  4. The service date(s). Which visit(s) this invoice covers. For recurring clients, invoice per visit or list each visit on a weekly/monthly invoice.
  5. Itemized services. One line per service — "Standard clean", "Deep clean — first visit", "Inside oven (add-on)" — each with its price. Itemizing is what prevents "why is it this much?" emails.
  6. Subtotal, tax (if you charge it), and the total due. Make the total impossible to miss. Check your local rules on whether cleaning services are taxable where you work.
  7. Payment terms and due date. "Due on receipt" is normal for residential cleaning. Whatever your terms, state a real date, not just "net 7".
  8. How to pay. List the exact methods you accept (card, bank transfer, payment app) with the details or link. Every extra step a client has to figure out delays payment.
  9. A late-fee line, if you use one. Only enforceable if the client agreed to it up front — put it in your service agreement first, then reference it here.
  10. A thank-you and next visit. One friendly line ("Thanks — see you on the 14th") turns a bill into a touchpoint and quietly confirms the schedule.

When should I send the invoice?

For one-time jobs (deep cleans, move-out cleans), send it the moment the job is done — while the clean is fresh and the client is happiest. Same-day invoices get paid dramatically faster than end-of-week batches. For recurring clients, pick one rhythm — per visit, or one itemized invoice weekly or monthly — and keep it consistent so payment becomes a habit on their side too.

Invoice, quote, or receipt — which is which?

A quote proposes a price before the work (anchor it with the free price calculator). The invoice requests payment after the work. A receipt confirms the payment landed. Small cleaning businesses genuinely need the first two; a receipt can be as simple as replying "received, thank you" or marking the invoice paid.

What if a client pays late?

First, make paying easy — a payment link beats bank details typed into an email. Second, put your late-fee policy in the service agreement the client signs before the first visit, so the line on your invoice is a reminder, not a surprise. Then a short friendly nudge at 3–5 days overdue ("resending in case it got buried") resolves the vast majority of late payments without conflict.

Can I use this template commercially?

Yes — copy it, adapt it, and bill your own clients with it. It's a starting skeleton, not tax or legal advice; check local rules on sales tax and record-keeping.

Do I need invoicing software?

Not on day one. A numbered, itemized invoice sent promptly — even as a plain email — beats software you haven't set up. Once you're juggling many recurring clients, software earns its keep with automatic reminders and payment links.

How do I number invoices?

Any consistent, ascending scheme: 001, 002… or year-based like 2026-014. Never reuse a number. If you void one, keep the number and mark it void — gaps are fine, duplicates are not.
