How to Start a House Cleaning Business

You don't need a franchise, a van, or a big loan to start cleaning homes for money — plenty of full-time cleaners started with a bucket, a car, and one client. What separates the ones who last from the ones who quit in three months isn't cleaning skill. It's pricing, paperwork, and getting a second client. Here's the practical, honest version — with free tools for each step.

Step 1

Decide your services & who you serve

Don't try to do everything on day one. The three most common residential services are standard recurring cleans (your bread and butter), deep cleans (higher-priced first visits), and move-in / move-out cleans (the priciest, often for landlords and real-estate agents). Pick the ones you can do well and say no to the rest until you have a system.

  • Recurring clients are the goal — predictable income beats a calendar full of one-offs.
  • Pick a service area you can drive without eating your profit in gas and time.
  • Niche down if you can — "move-out cleans for property managers" is easier to market than "cleaning."
Step 2

Work out what to charge

Underpricing is the number-one reason new cleaners burn out — they fill their week and still can't pay their bills. Most independent cleaners in the U.S. charge roughly $0.08–$0.13 per square foot or $25–$50 an hour, with a job minimum around $100–$120. Deep cleans run 1.5–2× a standard clean; move-outs more.

Price revenue, not profit

The number a client pays isn't what you keep. Before you set your rate, subtract supplies, gas and drive time, insurance, payment fees, and taxes. Know your target take-home per hour and quote so you actually hit it.

Step 3

Do the boring-but-critical legal setup

This part isn't glamorous, but skipping it is how people get burned. Requirements vary by state and country, so treat this as a checklist to research locally, not legal advice:

  • Pick a name you won't regret — browse 150+ cleaning business name ideas plus a 60-second availability checklist (state registry, domain, social handles) before you print anything.
  • Register your business — many start as a sole proprietor and form an LLC once income is steady, for liability protection.
  • Get liability insurance — a client's broken lamp or a scratched floor shouldn't come out of your pocket. It's cheaper than most people expect and it lets you charge more.
  • Open a separate bank account for the business so your books stay clean at tax time.
  • Check local licensing — some cities require a business license even for solo operators.

Step 5

Buy your starter supplies

You can start with a surprisingly short list and add as you go. A reliable starter kit: microfiber cloths (lots), a good all-purpose cleaner, glass cleaner, bathroom/disinfectant cleaner, a vacuum, a mop and bucket, scrub pads, gloves, and a sturdy caddy to carry it room to room. The full cleaning supplies list breaks this into a printable shopping list with a realistic starter budget (and what not to buy yet).

  • Buy concentrate where you can — it's cheaper per job than ready-to-use sprays.
  • Keep a "products to avoid" note per client (finishes, allergies, pets) — your intake form captures this.
  • Track supply cost per job so your pricing stays honest as you scale.
Step 6

Land your first 3 clients

Your first clients almost never come from ads — they come from people who already trust you. Start there, then build a repeatable channel:

  • Tell everyone you know you're taking cleaning clients — friends, family, neighbors, your old coworkers.
  • Post in local groups (neighborhood apps, community Facebook groups) where self-promo is allowed, and ask happy clients to refer you.
  • Set up a free Google Business Profile so you show up in local "house cleaner near me" searches.
  • Offer a small first-clean incentive to convert a "maybe" into a booking — then win them on quality, not price.

Three reliable recurring clients is often enough to prove the model and decide whether to go full-time.

Step 7

Look professional from visit one

Clients don't hire the best cleaner — they hire the one who feels the most trustworthy and organized. A written quote, a signed agreement, a tidy intake process, on-time arrival, and a consistent checklist do more for repeat business than a spotless baseboard nobody noticed. This is exactly where solo cleaners lose to franchises, and exactly where a little paperwork closes the gap.

Step 8

Get paid, then get repeat business

Send a clear invoice on completion, make it easy to pay (card, transfer, app), and state your terms in advance so late payment is rare. Then turn one job into many: put recurring clients on a fixed schedule, ask for a review and a referral while you're standing in their clean kitchen, and keep every client's preferences on file so each visit feels effortless for them.

How much does it cost to start a house cleaning business?

Less than almost any other business. Many solo cleaners start for under $200 in supplies plus insurance, using a car they already own. Your biggest early investments are time and professionalism, not equipment. Start lean, reinvest your first profits into better supplies and marketing, and only take on bigger costs (staff, a second vehicle, software) once recurring revenue justifies them.

Do I need a license or LLC to clean houses?

It depends on where you live. Many cleaners begin as sole proprietors and form an LLC once income is steady, mostly for liability protection. Some cities require a general business license. Check your local rules — and get liability insurance regardless, because it protects you and lets you charge like a professional. (This is general guidance, not legal advice.)

How do I get clients with no reviews yet?

Start with warm contacts and local groups, over-deliver on the first few jobs, and ask every satisfied client for a short review and a referral. A handful of genuine reviews plus a free Google Business Profile is usually enough to start showing up in local searches. Professionalism — a written quote, a signed agreement, showing up on time — builds trust faster than any ad.

What should I charge for my first job?

Use the free House Cleaning Price Calculator to get a fair range for the specific home, then don't discount below your job minimum. First-time and deep cleans should always be your highest rate.

What paperwork do I actually need on day one?

At minimum: a simple service agreement, an intake form, and an invoice. Checklists keep your work consistent. All of these (editable) are in the Complete House Cleaning Business Pack.

Standard, deep, or move-out — which should I offer first?

Standard recurring cleans give you predictable income and are the foundation. Deep and move-out cleans pay more per job but are less predictable — add them once you have a system and a checklist for each.
