Cleaning Business Name Ideas — 150+ Names, and How to Pick One You Won't Regret

Browse names by the style you want your business to feel like, then run the 60-second availability checklist before you print a single flyer. Naming is the fun part of starting a cleaning business — this page keeps it fun and keeps you out of a rename later.

The 5 rules of a good cleaning business name

  1. Say what you do. "Fresh Nest Cleaning" needs no explanation; "Fresh Nest Solutions" does. Clients search for the word cleaning (or maid, or housekeeping) — having it in the name helps them, and helps you show up when they search.
  2. Pass the phone test. Say it out loud as if answering a call: "Good morning, [name]!" If it's a mouthful, or you have to spell it ("that's Kleen with a K…"), keep looking. Cute spellings cost you word-of-mouth referrals — the person being told the name can't find you.
  3. Leave room to grow. "Downtown Apartment Cleaning" is limiting the day you take a suburban house client. Anchor to a feeling or a place-sized area, not to one service or one street.
  4. Check it's actually available. Not just the domain — the state business registry, and whether another cleaner two towns over already ranks for it. The checklist below takes a minute and saves a rebrand.
  5. Pick the one you'll still like saying in year three. A funny name is a real strategy (people remember it) — but only if you'll enjoy the joke on your 500th phone call.

The 60-second availability checklist

Run every finalist through all five. Do this before you order magnets, print quotes, or open a bank account.

  • Google it in quotes + your state ("Fresh Nest Cleaning" Ohio). Another cleaner with the same name in your market means move on — you'd be marketing for them.
  • State business registry. Search your Secretary of State's business-name database. If it's registered in your state, it's off the table (and registering yours protects it).
  • Domain. Check yourname.com at any registrar. The .com taken by a parked page is livable; taken by another cleaning company is not. Hyphens and "llc" bolted on are last resorts.
  • Social handles. Facebook and Instagram at minimum — local cleaning clients genuinely do find cleaners there. A matching handle everywhere beats a clever name available nowhere.
  • Say it over the phone to one person. If they can spell it and repeat it back, it passes. This catches everything the other four miss.

How the pros actually pick: the shortlist method

Don't hunt for "the one" — collect ten you like from the lists above, then eliminate. Cut anything that fails the phone test, run the survivors through the availability checklist, and you'll usually have two or three left. Pick the one that matches the clients you want: "Velvet Broom" and "Grime Scene Cleaners" can both be great businesses, but they book very different houses at very different rates (the start-a-cleaning-business guide covers choosing your market before your name).

Match the name to your price point

Your name sets a price expectation before you say a number. Luxury names support premium rates but raise the bar on everything else looking polished — your quotes, your invoices, your uniform. Friendly and funny names win volume and referrals in middle-market neighborhoods. There's no wrong tier, but a mismatch (luxury name, bargain rates — or the reverse) confuses clients and costs bookings.

Do you need "LLC" in the name?

On legal paperwork, yes if you form one — on your logo and car magnet, no. Most owners register "Fresh Nest Cleaning LLC" and brand as "Fresh Nest Cleaning". If you operate under any name other than your own legal/registered one, that's a DBA ("doing business as") filing — usually cheap and done at the county or state level. Rules vary by state; your Secretary of State's site will say.

Should I put my city in the name?

It's the strongest move for search — people literally type "house cleaning [town]" — at the cost of flexibility if you expand. A middle path: keep the brand city-free and put the city everywhere else (tagline, website title, Google Business Profile), which gets most of the search benefit without the lock-in.

Is "maid" outdated?

It's still one of the most-searched words in the industry, so it earns its keep in a name. Some owners prefer "home cleaning" or "housekeeping" for tone. Go with what your target clients would type — search interest for "maid service" remains strong in most US markets.

Can I rename my cleaning business later?

Yes — new DBA, updated registrations, new reviews profile — but you'll pay in lost review history and word-of-mouth momentum, which are a young cleaning business's biggest assets. An hour with the checklist above now is much cheaper than a rebrand in year two.

Are these names free to use?

These are ideas and starting points, freely offered — but availability is everything. Any name here may already be in use somewhere; that's exactly what the 60-second checklist is for. Availability in your state and market is what makes a name yours.
