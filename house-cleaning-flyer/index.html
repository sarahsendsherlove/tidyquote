What every cleaning flyer needs (in this order)

  • A headline about them, not you. "Come home to a clean house" beats "Smith Cleaning Services LLC" every time. Your business name goes at the bottom.
  • Your area, named. "Serving Maple Grove & Riverside" tells a reader in seconds that you're local — the #1 thing flyer readers are screening for.
  • Exactly three services. Standard, deep, move-out. More options = more hesitation.
  • One concrete offer with scarcity that's true. "$25 off your first clean — 3 spots this month" works because it's specific and honest. Never fake the scarcity; you'll be posting in the same five places for years.
  • A trust line. Licensed & insured (only if true — see the legal setup step), supplies included, fast quote promise.
  • A phone number readable from six feet away. Texting beats calling for this generation of clients — say "text for a quote."
  • Tear-offs or a QR code. Nobody photographs a corkboard. Give them something to take.

Where to post it (legally) — and where not to

The reliable five

Community corkboards at coffee shops, laundromats, grocery stores, libraries, and gyms — ask staff first, and they almost always say yes. Add vet offices and daycares once you have a review or two: both are full of busy households that outsource cleaning. Re-visit every two weeks; boards get cleared.

Door-to-door, done right

Door hangers and flyers tucked in the door frame are legal in most places. Never put anything in or on a mailbox — in the U.S. the mailbox is federally reserved for stamped mail (18 U.S.C. § 1725) and violations carry real fines. Check whether your city requires a solicitation permit for door-to-door distribution, and skip homes with "no soliciting" signs — those become complaint calls, not clients.

How many, and what to expect

Be honest with yourself about the math: printed flyers typically convert somewhere around 0.5–2%, so 200 well-placed flyers might produce one to four inquiries. That sounds bleak until you remember one weekly client is worth $5,000+ a year — a $20 print run that lands a single recurring client is the best marketing ROI you'll ever get. Track which location each call mentions (use a different offer code per spot if you're ambitious) and double down where the calls come from.

Print cheap, look sharp

Black ink on bright colored paper (yellow, green) often out-pulls full color for corkboard flyers — it stands out and costs pennies a page at any print shop or library. Save full-color card stock for door hangers in neighborhoods you specifically want. And before you print 500: print 20, post them, and see if the phone buzzes. Treat the flyer like a quote — revise and resend.

Flyers vs. Facebook groups vs. Nextdoor — which first?

Do all three with the same message — the flyer text above works verbatim as a neighborhood-group post. Online posts are free and instant but scroll away in a day; a corkboard flyer works for weeks. The winning combo for most new cleaners: online post for the fast start, flyers for the steady drip, and a Google Business Profile so "cleaner near me" searches find you.

Should I put prices on the flyer?

Put the offer ("$25 off your first clean"), not your rate card. Prices on a flyer age badly and anchor conversations before you've seen the house. Quote after a quick walk-through or a text exchange with photos — the calculator gives you the range in seconds.

Can I say "licensed & insured" yet?

Only if both are true. If you're not there yet, drop the line rather than fudge it — "Local & independent · Supplies included" is honest and still builds trust. Then handle the legal setup; liability insurance for a solo cleaner is usually cheaper than people expect.

What about flyers on parked cars?

Skip it. It's banned in many cities, tolerated in few, and the association is "gym sale," not "person I'll let into my home." A cleaning business sells trust; post where trusted local businesses post.
