Why every cleaning business needs an intake form

The difference between a cleaner who looks professional and one who doesn't is usually the first ten minutes. A simple intake form does three things at once: it makes you look organized and trustworthy, it lets you quote accurately (square footage, type of clean, add-ons), and it surfaces the surprises — pets, allergies, a no-touch antique, a tricky lockbox — before they become a problem on the job.

What to ask a new cleaning client

At minimum, capture: contact details, the home's size and layout, the exact service and frequency they want, how you'll get in, pets and allergies, anything to avoid, and the quoted price. The template above covers all of it — fill one out for every new client and keep it on file. Re-confirm the details at the first visit; homes and priorities change.

Turn the intake into a quote and a contract

Once you have the home's details, price the job with the free House Cleaning Price Calculator, then put the number in writing with a quote and a short service agreement. Clients hire the cleaner who makes the whole thing feel easy and clear — that paperwork is exactly what the full pack provides.

Should I use a paper form or a digital one?

Either works — the questions matter more than the format. Many solo cleaners print this form for in-person walk-throughs and keep a photo of the completed sheet per client. If you prefer digital, the editable version in the pack can be filled on a phone or laptop.

Do I need this before the first clean?

Yes — ideally before you even quote. The intake is what makes your quote accurate and your first visit smooth. It only takes a few minutes and prevents the most common first-job disputes (wrong scope, missed allergy, no access).
