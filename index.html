How much should a house cleaning cost?

Most independent cleaners in the U.S. charge somewhere between $0.08 and $0.13 per square foot for a standard clean, or roughly $25–$50 per hour. A typical 1,500 sq ft home runs about $120–$200 for a standard recurring clean, and more for a first-time deep clean. The calculator above uses these common ranges as a starting point — your real number depends on your market, your costs, and how fast you work.

Standard vs. deep vs. move-out

A standard clean maintains an already-cared-for home. A deep clean (often the first visit with a new client) costs roughly 1.5–2× more because it covers build-up: baseboards, inside appliances, detailed bathrooms. A move-in/move-out clean is usually the priciest — the home is empty, every surface is in play, and it's often inspected by a landlord.

Why recurring clients pay less per visit

A weekly or biweekly client is predictable income and faster to clean once you know the home, so it's normal to discount the per-visit price 10–20% versus a one-time job. You make it up in volume and stability. One-time and first-time deep cleans should always be your highest rate.

Don't forget your real costs

The price the calculator shows is revenue, not profit. Before you quote, subtract supplies, travel and gas, insurance, payment fees, and your own taxes. A good rule: know your target hourly take-home, estimate the hours honestly (including the drive), and never quote below your job minimum — most pros set one around $100–$120.

How to actually win the job

Price is only half of it. Clients hire the cleaner who looks organized and trustworthy: a clear written quote, a simple service agreement, and a tidy intake process. That's exactly what most solo cleaners are missing — and what the pack above is built to fix.

Is this calculator accurate for my area?

It uses national-average U.S. ranges. High cost-of-living metros (e.g. coastal cities) often run 20–40% higher; rural areas can run lower. Treat the number as a starting anchor and adjust to what your local market will bear.

Should I charge hourly or by the job?

Flat per-job pricing is almost always better once you're experienced — clients prefer a known number, and you're rewarded for working efficiently. Hourly makes sense only for unpredictable first-time or hoarding/post-renovation jobs.
